When nature strikes:
Australia's disaster landscape

Australia is one of the world's most disaster-prone nations — battered by bushfires, floods, cyclones and heatwaves year after year. As the climate warms, these events are becoming more frequent and more costly. This visualisation explores six decades of natural disaster data to reveal where, when and how hard nature hits.

Section 1 — The big picture

Disasters are becoming more frequent

Since 1960, Australia has recorded over 230 significant natural disasters. Storms and cyclones are the most common, but floods and bushfires cause disproportionate deaths. The 2010s saw a sharp rise in recorded events, reflecting both more extreme weather and improved reporting.

Economic losses have grown dramatically since the 1980s. The 2022 south-east Queensland and NSW floods caused A$8.25 billion in insured losses alone — the costliest flood event in Australian history.

Section 2 — Geography of disaster

Queensland bears the heaviest burden

Queensland experiences more insured catastrophes than any other state, driven by its tropical cyclone season in the north and recurring floods across the southeast. The map below shows total insured losses by state since 1980 — Queensland accounts for over 70% of the national total.

Insured losses normalised to 2022 AUD; states with no ICA data shown in grey.

The two main disaster types follow distinct geographic patterns. Bushfires concentrate in the southern states — particularly Victoria and New South Wales, where dry summers and eucalypt forests create ideal burning conditions. Floods cluster along the eastern seaboard, driven by tropical systems and La Niña rainfall.

Multi-state events are counted for each state affected.

Tropical cyclones form in warm waters north and northwest of Australia, then track south and east — many making landfall along the Queensland and Western Australian coasts.

Section 3 — Bushfires

Bushfires: deadly, costly and intensifying

Bushfires have claimed more Australian lives than any other disaster type per event. The 2009 Black Saturday fires in Victoria killed 173 people — Australia's deadliest peacetime disaster. The 2019–20 Black Summer fires burned 18.6 million hectares and caused an estimated A$4.4 billion in damage.

Section 4 — Floods and cyclones

Floods cause the most economic damage

While cyclones are most frequent, floods consistently cause the greatest economic losses. The 2022 floods were triggered by a rare triple La Niña event, inundating hundreds of communities across southeastern Australia. Cyclones follow a strong seasonal pattern, peaking from December to March.

Section 5 — The climate connection

A warming Australia means more disasters

Australia has warmed by 1.51°C since national records began in 1910. The warmest years on record have all occurred since 2013. As temperatures rise, fire weather becomes more dangerous, rainfall events more intense, and heatwaves more deadly.

Bars show temperature anomaly relative to 1961–1990 baseline (red = above average, blue = below). Purple line shows annual disaster count.

Heatwaves are Australia's deadliest hazard when measured by total excess mortality. The 2009 south-east Australian heatwave killed 347 people — more than the Black Saturday fires that followed days later.

Deaths from recorded heatwave events. The 2009 Victorian heatwave remains Australia's deadliest.
